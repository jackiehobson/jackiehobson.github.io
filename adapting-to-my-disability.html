Adapting to my disability

March 03, 2023
 · 2 min read
 · Jackie Hobson

When I was a young child, I had to play this really boring video game. If a box popped up, or I heard a specific sound, I had to press a button. The faster and more accurately I pressed the button, the better I did. Yet no matter how hard I tried, I never got a score good enough. The video game is called the "Test of Variables of Attention" (TOVA), and it's what psychiatrists use to help diagnose and monitor treatment of a patient with ADHD.

Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a cognitive disability where the patient has trouble with inattentive and/or hyperactive behavior. Trouble focusing on schoolwork and staying still are two common symptoms. The symptoms can be mild to severe. In my case, I have a hard time focusing and sitting still all the time.

When I was diagnosed with ADHD as a child, some things became easier to understand, like why I acted differently than other kids or why I had more of a problem with certain subjects. Adjusting to my diagnosis was a troublesome issue. I felt like I wasn't intelligent and my already poor self esteem was worsened. I was put on stimulant medication alongside therapy. It made me feel different, and not my usual self. I wasn't really bothered by this. The insomnia and lack of appetite was different though. Adapting to the stimulant medication was hard, so hard that I was unable to overcome the side effects and quit.

My symptoms didn't get any better, especially now that I wasn't medicated. Eventually I became a teenager, and I started taking a different medication. This medication didn't have any side effects compared to my last one, which was a relief. I was able to succeed in high school, thanks to my medication and therapy.

Yet, I still had problems with how I felt about my diagnosis. I took it personally as a slight against my identity and intelligence. My self esteem suffered due to it, and when I had trouble with a task I became bitter and self-destructive. Through therapy, I was able to accept that my diagnosis is only one small part of my identity, and that ultimately I make my own identity, not my disability.

Medication is different for every patient for the most part. Medication can stop working as well. In my case, mine stopped working when I got to college. This was extremely problematic as I had just entered a stressful, academically demanding part of my now adult life. I tried multiple different medications until I found the one right for me; with maximized theraputic effects and minimized side effects.

Becoming an independent adult with ADHD in college is hard. Juggling schoolwork, therapy, medication, self care, a proper routine and sleep schedule is stressful. I've learned that keeping medication and therapy on a regular routine is essential to my success and progress. Becoming independent this past few years has been hard, but ultimately greatly rewarding.

Adapting to my ADHD diagnosis is a long and arduous process that is still on going today. The hard work and dedication has paid off with a wonderfully rewarding adult life.
